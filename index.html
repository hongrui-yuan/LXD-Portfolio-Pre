Residency context

My residency took place at Fast Forward Medical Innovation (FFMI), where I supported projects related to online commercialization education. Much of my work centered on fastPACE-related course materials and gradually moved from reviewing existing learning experiences to building learner-facing supports and design resources in Canvas.

What I worked on

My residency followed a full cycle from review to design. I began by examining existing FFMI course materials, identifying gaps in structure, alignment, and learner support, and conducting external research to better understand the commercialization content behind the courses. As the work developed, my role moved beyond analysis into design. I contributed to templates, learner-facing tools, and course support materials, and I began thinking more directly about how Canvas could be used to reorganize content, add missing supports, and strengthen the learner experience. This portfolio documents that progression through a selected set of representative artifacts.

How this work strengthened my LXD competencies

Through this residency, I came to see design more clearly as a process of translation. Review work showed me where a course felt unclear, incomplete, or difficult to act on, but design work required me to respond to those gaps in more concrete ways. By moving into templates, learner-facing tools, and Canvas-based course support, I strengthened my ability to connect analysis with action. This experience helped me grow not only in reviewing learning environments, but also in designing structures that make complex content more usable, more navigable, and more instructionally coherent for learners.

Featured Artifact 1

fastPACE Precursor Pilot Review

Reviewing alignment, scaffolding, and learner guidance in a pre-launch online course

Applying theories Research & evaluation Inclusive design Technology affordances
What it is

This artifact is a pilot review of the fastPACE Precursor course, an early-stage online learning experience designed to help learners clarify an innovation need before entering the full fastPACE program.

How it was developed

I reviewed the unpublished course alongside its learning goals and completed a structured feedback template module by module. My analysis focused on alignment, scaffolding, navigation clarity, assessment guidance, and the learner experience within an e-learning environment.

My contribution

I identified recurring issues such as uneven alignment between ambitious learning outcomes and lighter supports, limited success criteria for assignments, unclear onboarding and navigation cues, and the course’s heavy reliance on text and video without enough alternative representations.

What I learned

This was my first experience systematically reviewing a course from a learning design perspective. I learned to ask not only whether content was present, but whether learners were actually being prepared to do the work the course expected of them.

View full artifact
Key takeaway

Strong learning outcomes need equally visible scaffolds, criteria, and learner guidance.

Featured Artifact 2

Commercialization Timeline and Gap Analysis

Building a learner-facing medical device development timeline to strengthen fastPACE course support

Research & evaluation Documentation Applying theories Workplace collaboration
What it is

This artifact is a commercialization timeline and gap analysis developed to support improvements to the published fastPACE Fall 2025 course. It began with a review of the existing Canvas course and then expanded into a research-informed medical device timeline used to identify missing or under-supported content.

How it was developed

I first reviewed the published fastPACE Fall 2025 Canvas course to understand its content, module structure, and overall sequence. I then used external research to map commercialization milestones across areas such as medical devices and therapeutics. After feedback, I narrowed the work to medical devices and revised the timeline to make each phase more explanatory and more useful for learners, while also identifying gaps between the timeline and the existing course materials.

My contribution

I reviewed course content, synthesized external sources, refined milestone phases, identified gaps between the timeline and Canvas modules, and added resources and definitions that could support learner understanding.

What I learned

This artifact taught me that LXD work sometimes requires stepping far outside one’s original content comfort zone. Even without being a domain expert, I could contribute by identifying what learners may not understand and where complex content needed clearer structure and support.

Key takeaway

Featured Artifact 3

Integrated Gap Analysis and Learning Goals

Turning distributed observations into shared instructional priorities for FFMI Canvas medical device phases

Workplace skills Research & evaluation Applying theories Documentation
What it is

This artifact is a collaborative gap analysis and learning goals document that brought together earlier individual analyses and translated shared gaps into learner-facing priorities and observable learning goals.

How it was developed

After conducting earlier individual reviews, Charles and I met to compare the gaps we had identified and discuss where our findings overlapped or differed. We consolidated the most important issues into nine integrated gaps and then defined foundational, applied, and performance-based learning goals for each.

My contribution

I helped synthesize prior findings, refine the structure of the gaps, raise questions about audience assumptions, and shape the learning goals connected to each gap. This work moved us from identifying missing content to defining what learners should actually be able to understand and do.

What I learned

I learned that gap analysis becomes much more useful when it is tied to learner performance rather than only missing content. I also became more aware that audience assumptions shape what truly needs explanation and what level of support is instructionally appropriate.

View full artifact
Key takeaway

The strongest design move is not only finding what is missing, but deciding what learners should be able to explain, produce, or justify when that gap is addressed.

Featured Artifact 6

Regulatory Affairs Additions for Medical Device Commercialization

Reframing regulation as an early strategy question within medical device commercialization learning

Applying theories Design resources Research & evaluation Technology affordances
What it is

This artifact is a design document that adds a dedicated medical device regulation and regulatory affairs section and revises existing learning supports for market analysis, intellectual property, and monetization so that they better reflect how device class and pathway shape commercialization decisions.

How it was developed

This artifact grew out of feedback on the earlier commercial learning tasks. Building on the previous market, IP, and monetization tools, the next step was to make early regulatory awareness more visible. The document introduced a learner-facing regulation section focused on intended use, user, setting, body contact, risk, predicate existence, plausible FDA class, likely pathway, and remaining uncertainty. It also reframed the earlier sections so that learners would consider not only who buys or pays, but also how regulatory burden affects market realism, time to market, and evidence expectations.

My contribution

I helped extend the earlier commercialization learning supports by incorporating regulatory logic into them more explicitly. This meant treating regulation not as a separate compliance topic, but as part of early strategy and instructional framing.

What I learned

This artifact helped me understand more clearly that strong learning design in professional education is often about reframing the sequence of thinking. In this case, the key issue was not just adding more information, but helping learners ask better questions earlier.

View full artifact
Key takeaway

In medical device commercialization, regulation is not a late-stage add-on. It shapes what becomes realistic, what evidence matters, and what decisions learners need to make early.

Applying Learning Theories and Design Frameworks

This competency first became visible in my course review work, where I examined whether learning outcomes, activities, and assessments were aligned and whether learners had enough scaffolding to complete expected work. Over time, I moved beyond identifying weak alignment and began defining learning goals, learner performances, and more intentional supports across later artifacts.

Creating Design Resources and Project Documentation

What began as review notes, checklists, and visual mappings gradually became a more deliberate part of my design practice. I learned that documentation does not only record decisions. It can also clarify thinking, surface gaps, support stakeholder communication, and become a bridge from analysis into design.

Fostering Workplace Skills and Professional Relationships

Because my residency was fully remote, collaboration depended on clear written communication, follow-up notes, revision cycles, and the ability to keep work moving between meetings. I became more intentional about making communication actionable and about treating feedback as part of the design process rather than as an administrative add-on.

Integrating Inclusive Design Principles and Practices

My understanding of inclusive design became much more practical through course review. Instead of thinking about accessibility only as compliance, I began using UDL and learner variability as review lenses, especially when documenting text-heavy lessons, unclear prompts, missing alternate supports, and places where the learner path could be made easier to follow.

Using Knowledge of Technology Affordances and Constraints

This competency appeared more broadly in my work than a simple checkbox suggests. Canvas was not just the platform where the course lived. Its navigation flow, media behavior, linked tools, and learner-facing structure shaped the experience itself. I also became more critical about how technologies such as generative AI can support early-stage research and idea organization while still requiring verification and judgment.

Using Research and Evaluation Skills

I grew from using outside sources to supplement my understanding into using them to justify design direction. External timelines, FDA materials, and commercialization references became tools for identifying what was missing, clarifying course phases, and making more evidence-informed recommendations about what learners should know, do, and be prepared for.

Final reflection

Looking across these artifacts, I now understand my residency less as a set of separate tasks and more as a connected design trajectory. What began as reviewing course materials and noticing learner-facing problems gradually developed into a more layered form of practice: analyzing structure, comparing content against external references, collaborating to define instructional priorities, and designing tools that could better support learner performance. This progression helped me see that learning experience design is not only about producing polished materials. It is also about shaping the conditions under which learning becomes clearer, more actionable, and more meaningful.

How my design thinking evolved

Early in the residency, I often approached design work by looking for visible problems: where learning goals and activities did not align, where scaffolding was thin, or where the learner path felt confusing. Over time, my thinking became more layered. I began asking not only what was missing, but what learners were actually being asked to understand, produce, justify, or decide. That shift moved me from a surface-level review mindset into a stronger design mindset. Concepts such as alignment, scaffolding, learner support, and representational access became more than course ideas to reference; they became practical lenses I used to evaluate real instructional materials in Canvas and to make decisions about what kinds of supports would meaningfully improve the experience. In that sense, this residency helped me move from recognizing design principles in theory to using them as working tools in practice.

What this residency changed in how I understand LXD

I also understand the LXD core competencies more concretely now because I experienced how they overlap in practice. Applying learning theories and design frameworks shaped how I interpreted alignment and learner support. Research and evaluation skills became important when I compared course content against external timelines, regulatory references, and development processes. Documentation and design resources emerged when gaps were translated into structured tools, templates, and task supports. Workplace skills were not separate from design work either; they were visible in feedback conversations, revision cycles, collaborative synthesis, and shared decision-making. Most importantly, I now see that learning experience design is not limited to building final products. It includes reviewing, clarifying, sequencing, structuring, and making learner thinking visible. Even when working outside my original domain knowledge, I can still contribute meaningfully by asking what learners need, what the environment currently affords, and how design can reduce unnecessary ambiguity while deepening engagement.
